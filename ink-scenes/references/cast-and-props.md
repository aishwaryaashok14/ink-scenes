# Cast and Props

## The robots

Robots play software, AI, servers, processes, machines — anything computational. They are the style's soul, so get them right:

- **Retro-friendly, not sleek**: boxy or rounded panels, visible joints, segmented limbs, a few cables or vents. Closer to a 1960s appliance or a friendly workshop droid than to a glossy android.
- **Matte and muted**: warm gray / putty bodies; one robot per scene may wear the accent color (helmet, torso, single panel) when the robot is the story's pivot.
- **Expressive through posture, not face**: faces are minimal — two dot eyes, a simple mouth line or a small screen. Feeling comes from body language: hunched over a keyboard, proudly holding out a paper bag, pointing patiently into the distance.
- **Gravely employed**: robots take mundane jobs completely seriously — chef, clerk, librarian, scribe. The deadpan comes from a machine doing a human chore with total commitment.
- **Scale tells the story**: a giant robot towering over a person reads as a powerful model; a small robot on a stool reading instructions reads as a process following its config. Use scale deliberately.
- Multiple arms, extra tool-limbs, or a head-mounted attachment are fine when the concept calls for parallelism or multitasking — keep it mechanical-plausible, never eldritch.

### Robot don'ts

- Not chibi, not kawaii, no blush marks, no giant anime eyes.
- Not menacing: no red glowing eyes, no weapons, no looming threat.
- Not branded: no resemblance to specific famous robots.

## The humans

Humans play users, developers, bystanders — the people the technology happens to:

- Small, simply drawn, plainly dressed; less rendered than the robots and machines around them.
- Often tiny relative to the systems they face — that asymmetry is half the editorial voice.
- One may wear the red-orange accent (a hoodie, a jacket) when the human is the pivot instead of a machine.
- Gestures are simple and legible: looking up, throwing hands up, waiting, pointing.

## Object-only scenes

Many of the best images have no characters at all. Two objects in contrast, or one strange machine, can carry the whole metaphor — a laden dinner table next to a blender of purée, a teetering pile of marked-up papers next to a neat bound stack. Prefer object-only when characters would only be spectators.

## Prop pool

Everyday, slightly old-fashioned physical things — the metaphor source language:

- **Kitchen/food**: dinner table, blender, pots on a stove, drive-through window, paper bag of takeout, bread rolls, roast.
- **Paper/office**: stacks of documents, bound reports, rubber stamps, ledgers, ornate old books, scrolls, ink pots, typewriter-ish keyboards, desks.
- **Street/built world**: small old cars, kiosk windows, walls, roads, roadside weeds, utility boxes, doors.
- **Mechanisms**: sliders, dials, levers, funnels, conveyor belts, pneumatic tubes, filing cabinets.

Use 1-3 props per scene. The props should feel like they were bought at a flea market, not an Apple store — the contrast between cozy-analog objects and digital concepts *is* the joke.
