# Style DNA

## One sentence

Warm cream paper, fine sepia-black ink, quiet muted color with one red-orange heartbeat, a single everyday scene that *is* the concept — the feel of an editorial illustration in a beautifully printed book, never an infographic.

Like a comics artist with a dip pen explaining a server to their grandmother by drawing her kitchen.

## Must have

- Landscape concept illustration: default 16:9, allow 2:1 for wide quiet scenes or two-panel contrasts.
- **Warm cream / off-white background** (think unbleached paper, `#F0E8DB`): flat, uniform, no paper texture, no gradient, no vignette, no noise.
- **Fine ink linework** in dark sepia-black — near-black with a warm brown cast, visibly ink rather than pure `#000`: confident, clean, even-weight contour lines — closer to ligne claire than to a wobbly sketch. Detail and shadow come from **selective crosshatching and stippling**, not from gray fills or soft shading.
- **Muted palette**: warm grays, putty, cream for most surfaces; plus **one red-orange accent hue** (brick red to tomato) with a single dominant carrier — the car, the robot's helmet, the blender base, the hoodie. Quiet earth tones (sage green, tan, dusty amber, maroon) may fill food, books, and surfaces, all clearly hushed below the accent.
- **Wordless**: no titles, no labels, no captions, no arrows-with-text. Documents, screens, spines, and signs inside the scene show only **asemic scribble** — marks that read as "text" from a distance but spell nothing. Tiny incidental **numerals** are allowed when counting is the point (numbered queue tickets, a dial); words never.
- Generous negative space: large areas of untouched cream; the subject occupies roughly 50-60% of the canvas.
- Grounding is minimal: a thin contact line or sparse hatched shadow under objects; often no shadow at all.
- One image expresses one concept through one scene metaphor.

## Color

### Measured palette

Sampled from reference images — use these as anchors, not absolutes:

| Role | Hex | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cream ground | `#F0E8DB` | ~40% of any canvas; flat, uniform |
| Light putty | `#E7DFD1` | pale surfaces, paper, robot panels |
| Putty / warm gray | `#D4CEBF` | machines, furniture, clothing |
| Greige | `#C1B39D` | deeper neutral surfaces, wood, shadow-side fills |
| Ink line | `#1C120B` | near-black with a warm brown cast — never pure `#000` |
| **Accent red** | `#8B3C35` → `#DF4E31` | brick to tomato-vermilion; center ~`#C84B33`; highlights may lift to `#F2775F` |
| Maroon | `#773639` | berries, book spines — reads as an earth tone, not a second accent |
| Dusty amber | `#BC8A4D` / `#936945` | roast, bread, leather, old paper |
| Sage-olive | `#858570` | greens are nearly gray — barely saturated |

The earth tones sit close to the neutrals in saturation; only the accent red is allowed to be vivid.

### Structure

This is a **muted-world-plus-one-flare** scheme, not a duotone:

- **Cream** — the world's air. Most of the canvas.
- **Warm gray / putty** — machines, furniture, clothing, paper. Flat fills inside ink contours.
- **Red-orange** (the flare) — one accent *hue* with **one dominant carrier**: the element the metaphor pivots on. Small echoes of the same hue are authentic and welcome — a pinstripe on the tablecloth, a knob on the machine, the helmets of background robots — but exactly one carrier dominates. If the accent could be removed without weakening the story, it is on the wrong thing. **When the scene has a protagonist among peers, the accent marks the protagonist** (the one red robot in a gray crowd, the one red car at the window).
- **Quiet earth tones** — sage green, tan, dusty amber, maroon for food, books, bins, and large surfaces. Several may coexist (a laden dinner table earns its turkey-amber, bean-sage, and cranberry-maroon), but every one of them stays hushed — low saturation, never competing with the accent. When in doubt, leave it gray.

Never neon, never saturated blue/green/purple, never a second *loud* color.

The *discipline* is the rule; the *hue* is a setting. Red-orange is the default signature, but if the user asks for a different accent (a deep teal, an ochre, a wine red), swap it for that one color everywhere — same restraint, same single dominant carrier, same hushed earth tones around it. One flare per image, whatever its color.

## Perspective and rendering

- Comfortable three-quarter or straight-on views; isometric/axonometric works well for object-only scenes (tables, machines, stacks).
- Forms are described by line and hatch, not by lighting. No specular highlights, no soft drop shadows, no ambient occlusion look.
- Density can range from very sparse (two objects on cream) to richly crosshatched (a cluttered desk, an overgrown roadside) — but density must serve the metaphor, and at least one zone of the canvas always stays quiet.

## Never

- No pure white background, and no beige *texture* — flat cream only.
- No infographic, flowchart, diagram, or slide look.
- No readable words, labels, callouts, or titled panels (tiny numerals only when counting is the point).
- No commercial flat-vector style, no gradients, no glossy 3D.
- No neon or saturated palette; no second loud color.
- No chibi/kawaii cuteness; no menacing or dystopian robots.
- No photo-realism, no UI screenshots.
- No busy edge-to-edge composition with no quiet zone.

## Aesthetic direction

Aim for warm, deadpan, patient, a little melancholy-funny — the humor of a robot in a chef's hat taking its job seriously.

Avoid cute, frantic, sterile, or sinister.
