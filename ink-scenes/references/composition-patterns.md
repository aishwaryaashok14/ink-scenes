# Composition Patterns & Originality Rules

## Base scene structures

Pick one structure; do not mix.

### Two-panel contrast (before/after, with/without)

For: compaction, refactoring, caching, compression, raw vs. processed, manual vs. automated.

How: two halves of one canvas — left state, right state — separated by cream space, never by a drawn divider or arrow. The two halves rhyme visually (same angle, same scale) so the difference reads instantly. Example shape: a laden dinner table on the left, a blender of purée on a pedestal on the right.

### Hand-off scene (one thing serving another)

For: APIs, requests/responses, endpoints, client/server, queues.

How: two parties and an exchange — a robot chef passing a takeout bag through a kiosk window to a robot in a small car. The window/counter/hatch is the interface; the bag is the payload. Whoever initiates or receives can carry the accent.

### Scale encounter

For: models vs. users, big systems, context windows, infrastructure someone depends on.

How: one very large figure or structure, one small human looking up at it, lots of cream sky. The asymmetry is the message. Keep the big thing patient, not threatening.

### Worked desk (hidden mechanism)

For: what a process is "really doing" — agents, runtimes, workers, compilers, training.

How: one robot at a desk dense with analog tools (books, ink pots, papers, a keyboard), richly crosshatched, mid-task with multiple things in motion. Density is allowed here, but keep one quiet cream zone around the scene.

### Quiet state

For: idle, waiting, daemon, cache hit, a process reading its instructions.

How: one character, one object, one action — a robot on a stool reading a single sheet beside an enormous ornate book. Maximum whitespace, minimum props.

### Strange machine

For: transformations and pipelines — embedding, tokenization, build steps.

How: one invented analog contraption (funnel, press, slider, conveyor) with a visible in-state and out-state. The machine should look flea-market plausible, not sci-fi.

## How to invent a fresh metaphor

Invent a new metaphor from the current concept every time. Do not copy a metaphor from a previous image.

### Three steps

1. Name the concept's physical truth: what *actually happens*, stripped of jargon — "many things become one mushy thing", "you ask through a hatch and get a bag back", "a big helper points the way", "the same dish is kept warm to avoid re-cooking".
2. Find the domestic scene where that truth already lives: kitchen, drive-through, post office, library, laundry, garage, street market.
3. Cast it: decide whether a robot, a human, or the objects alone perform the truth — then give the red-orange accent to the pivot of the story.

### Scene pool

Kitchen and dinner table, drive-through and kiosk windows, libraries and ornate books, desks and paperwork, post and parcels, small old cars and streets, workshops, market stalls, laundromats, filing rooms.

Use one scene per image; never collage two scenes.

## Anti-cliché rules — and the shared-world exception

For one-off images, every concept deserves its own scene. Even similar concepts get different rooms of the house — if caching was a kept-warm dish, memoization should not be; maybe it is a rubber stamp that saves re-writing the same letter.

**The exception is a series.** When illustrating a *family* of related concepts, deliberately keep them in one coherent world so the metaphors compound: in a restaurant world, the ticket rail is the queue, one order ticket is a job, the chalkboard tally is state, and the walk-in fridge is the cache. A reader who got one image gets the next faster. Within the shared world, each scene still stages a fresh moment — same kitchen, never the same shot twice.

The point of the style is consistency of *voice and discipline* — cream, ink, one accent, wordless — not repetition of scenes. Robots and dinner tables are the cast and the stage, never the punchline itself.
