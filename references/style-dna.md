# Style DNA

## One sentence

Warm cream paper, fine sepia-black ink, quiet muted color with one red-orange heartbeat, a single everyday scene that *is* the concept — the feel of an editorial illustration in a beautifully printed book, never an infographic.

Like a comics artist with a dip pen explaining a server to their grandmother by drawing her kitchen.

## Must have

- Landscape concept illustration: default 16:9, allow 2:1 for wide quiet scenes or two-panel contrasts.
- **Warm cream / off-white background** (think unbleached paper, roughly `#EFEAE0`): flat, uniform, no paper texture, no gradient, no vignette, no noise.
- **Fine ink linework** in dark sepia-black: confident, clean, even-weight contour lines — closer to ligne claire than to a wobbly sketch. Detail and shadow come from **selective crosshatching and stippling**, not from gray fills or soft shading.
- **Muted palette**: warm grays, putty, cream for most surfaces; plus **exactly one red-orange accent** (brick red to tomato) on the element that carries the story — the car, the helmet, the book, the slider knob, the hoodie. Optionally one quiet secondary muted tone (sage green, tan, dusty amber) for a large surface, used so softly it never competes.
- **Completely wordless**: no titles, no labels, no captions, no arrows-with-text. Documents, screens, spines, and signs inside the scene show only **asemic scribble** — marks that read as "text" from a distance but spell nothing.
- Generous negative space: large areas of untouched cream; the subject occupies roughly 50-60% of the canvas.
- Grounding is minimal: a thin contact line or sparse hatched shadow under objects; often no shadow at all.
- One image expresses one concept through one scene metaphor.

## Color

This is a **muted-world-plus-one-flare** scheme, not a duotone:

- **Cream** — the world's air. Most of the canvas.
- **Warm gray / putty** — machines, furniture, clothing, paper. Flat fills inside ink contours.
- **Red-orange** (the flare) — exactly one element, and it should be the element the metaphor pivots on. If the accent could be removed without weakening the story, it is on the wrong thing.
- **Optional second muted tone** — one large quiet surface (a sage tabletop, a tan turkey). It must be clearly quieter than the accent. When in doubt, leave it gray.

Never neon, never saturated blue/green/purple, never more than one loud color.

## Perspective and rendering

- Comfortable three-quarter or straight-on views; isometric/axonometric works well for object-only scenes (tables, machines, stacks).
- Forms are described by line and hatch, not by lighting. No specular highlights, no soft drop shadows, no ambient occlusion look.
- Density can range from very sparse (two objects on cream) to richly crosshatched (a cluttered desk, an overgrown roadside) — but density must serve the metaphor, and at least one zone of the canvas always stays quiet.

## Never

- No pure white background, and no beige *texture* — flat cream only.
- No infographic, flowchart, diagram, or slide look.
- No readable text, labels, callouts, or titled panels.
- No commercial flat-vector style, no gradients, no glossy 3D.
- No neon or saturated palette; no second loud color.
- No chibi/kawaii cuteness; no menacing or dystopian robots.
- No photo-realism, no UI screenshots.
- No busy edge-to-edge composition with no quiet zone.

## Aesthetic direction

Aim for warm, deadpan, patient, a little melancholy-funny — the humor of a robot in a chef's hat taking its job seriously.

Avoid cute, frantic, sterile, or sinister.
